# EPIC 7 - Story 7.5

## BeatDrop Offline Visualizer Artifact Export

## Goal

Export offline visualizer-planning artifacts for BeatDrop-style playback so preset choice and transition timing are driven by analyzed musical structure instead of random preset changes.

This story is intentionally offline-only. It does not require live OSC, MIDI, hotkey injection, or any other runtime control path.

The target reference is the BeatDrop / MilkDrop-style preset workflow shown in:

- `https://github.com/OfficialIncubo/BeatDrop-Music-Visualizer`

## Inputs

- fixture-agnostic or fixture-aware lighting intent from Stories 7.3 and 7.4
- canonical beat and bar timing from Story 1.2
- FFT-band transient evidence from Story 1.3
- loudness and energy summaries from Story 1.4 and Story 4.1
- harmonic and symbolic section context from Stories 3.1, 3.4, and 7.1
- event timeline exports from Story 5.6 when they provide stronger transition semantics
- optional curated preset library metadata that tags presets with descriptors such as `aggressive`, `uplifting`, `percussive`, `ambient`, `psychedelic`, `strobe_safe`, or `hard_cut_friendly`

## Problem Framing

BeatDrop and MilkDrop-style visualizers are strong at rendering presets reactively, but they do not know the future structure of the analyzed song on their own.

This repo does know the future structure because section boundaries, drops, FFT transients, and energy ramps are already inferred offline. Story 7.5 uses that offline knowledge to prepare a deterministic preset schedule ahead of playback.

Two core use cases drive this story:

1. Feature-driven preset selection:
   Match a preset profile such as `aggressive` specifically to sections where high-energy FFT activity aligns with a context-aware high-payoff section such as a chorus-like `focal_lift`, `groove_plateau`, or similar strong state.
2. Predictive hard cuts:
   Prepare a visual preset transition ahead of time so the cut lands exactly on a predicted structural boundary that is later confirmed by a physical sub-band transient.

## Dependencies

- deterministic preset-profile scoring
- transition scheduling rules
- optional preset-library tagging or curation
- export packaging for downstream manual or adapter-based consumption

## Processing Steps

1. Load the canonical section timeline, cue anchors, FFT-band transients, and energy-derived intensity signals.
2. Score each section and subsection for visual intent dimensions such as aggression, motion, density, brightness, tension, release, and stability.
3. Match those dimensions against a curated visual preset library using explicit tags rather than random selection.
4. Build a per-section preset recommendation timeline with confidence and reason fields.
5. Detect upcoming structural boundaries that should trigger a preset change.
6. Mark a boundary as `hard_cut_ready` only when:
   - the system predicts a meaningful structural change ahead of time from the offline section and energy evidence,
   - the transition lands on an exact canonical cue anchor,
   - the nearest FFT-band onset or dropout confirms the physical timing strongly enough for zero-latency visual impact.
7. Emit an offline schedule artifact that records which preset should be active, when it should change, and why that choice was made.
8. Emit an optional human-readable companion markdown that explains the preset logic for operators or for a future adapter layer.

## Offline Export Contract

Story 7.5 must not assume undocumented BeatDrop internals.

The canonical output of this story is an offline planning package that a human, a small adapter script, or a future BeatDrop-specific bridge can consume later.

The export should prefer stable repo-owned artifacts such as:

- `data/output/<Song - Artist>/beatdrop_visual_plan.json`
- `data/output/<Song - Artist>/beatdrop_visual_plan.md`

If a future integration writes BeatDrop-native config files, those files must be treated as derived projections from the canonical offline plan rather than the primary source of truth.

## Preset Profile Vocabulary

The exporter should use a compact profile vocabulary that can be mapped onto real BeatDrop or MilkDrop presets:

- `aggressive`
- `anthemic`
- `uplifting`
- `percussive`
- `hypnotic`
- `ambient`
- `dreamy`
- `tense`
- `release`
- `hard_cut_friendly`
- `strobe_safe`

This vocabulary is intentionally descriptive rather than product-specific. The preset library may map multiple actual presets onto the same profile.

## Feature-Driven Selection Rules

- High FFT flux, strong loudness, dense accents, and chorus-like or payoff-like section semantics should increase `aggressive` and `anthemic` scores.
- Repetitive groove sections with sustained pulse but less structural shock should prefer `hypnotic`, `percussive`, or `uplifting` profiles depending on brightness and motion.
- Breath-space, intro, or release windows should suppress aggressive presets and favor `ambient`, `dreamy`, or `tense` profiles depending on harmonic and energy context.
- Repeated section types should not be forced into the exact same preset every time. Controlled variation is allowed when the mapped profile family stays consistent.
- Preset choice must preserve a safety filter so `strobe_safe` or intensity caps can be enforced globally.

## Predictive Hard Cut Rules

- The exporter may schedule a hard cut before playback because the full song has already been analyzed offline.
- A hard cut recommendation must reference a predicted future structural boundary, not only a reactive peak.
- The cut timestamp must resolve to the exact canonical anchor time used elsewhere in the pipeline.
- The boundary must be physically confirmed by a nearby FFT-band transient or dropout event.
- If the structural prediction is strong but the transient confirmation is weak, the export should fall back to a softer transition recommendation instead of claiming a hard cut.

## Acceptance Criteria

- The export is generated entirely from offline analysis artifacts.
- High-payoff sections with strong FFT and energy evidence are mapped to stronger preset profiles such as `aggressive` only when the evidence supports that choice.
- The system can schedule hard-cut recommendations at predicted structural boundaries before playback begins.
- Hard-cut timestamps align with canonical section or cue-anchor times and are confirmed by physical FFT evidence.
- When the evidence is ambiguous, the exporter degrades gracefully to a softer transition recommendation instead of inventing certainty.
- The output stays deterministic for the same song and preset-library metadata.
- The canonical export remains product-agnostic enough to survive BeatDrop implementation changes.

## Validation

- Compare recommended preset-profile shifts against section labels, energy windows, and FFT-band activity for a representative set of songs.
- Verify that every `hard_cut` transition references an exact anchor and a confirming transient or dropout.
- Verify that aggressive preset recommendations cluster around musically strong payoff windows rather than being evenly or randomly distributed.
- Confirm that repeated chorus-like sections preserve family-level consistency while still allowing controlled preset variation.
- Confirm that the offline plan can be read without access to any live playback runtime.

## Failure Cases

- Random or weakly justified preset assignment
- Treating any loud moment as a hard cut without structural prediction
- Scheduling transitions from reactive peaks alone and ignoring canonical boundaries
- Overfitting preset choice to one metric such as loudness while ignoring section semantics
- Writing BeatDrop-specific opaque files as the only source of truth

## Out of Scope

- Live visualizer control at playback time
- Real-time audio-reactive preset switching
- Keyboard, OSC, MIDI, or window automation
- Editing or bundling BeatDrop itself
