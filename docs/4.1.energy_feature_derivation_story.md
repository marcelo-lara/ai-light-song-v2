# EPIC 4 - Story 4.1

## Derived Energy Features

## Goal

Derive higher-level energy summaries from low-level energy features and section structure so downstream lighting logic can reason about intensity, buildup, release, and accent candidates.

## Inputs

- Low-level energy features from Story 2.6
- Section windows from Story 3.1
- Optional hints, rises, drops, or stem-aware envelopes when available

## Dependencies

- Rule-based aggregation across frames, beats, bars, and sections

## Processing Steps

1. Aggregate loudness, centroid, flux, and onset evidence into section-level energy cards.
2. Derive global energy statistics such as mean, peak, dynamic range, and trend.
3. Identify notable peaks, dips, builds, plateaus, and release windows.
4. Generate accent candidates from onset-rich windows and notable rises or drops.
5. Persist the result into the energy layer contract.

## Output Artifacts

- enriched `layer_c_energy.json`

## Acceptance Criteria

- Section energy cards capture the perceived energy arc of the song.
- Accent candidates are sparse enough to be useful but dense enough to mark real musical moments.
- Peaks, dips, and trend labels are derived from the actual energy signal rather than hand-authored labels.

## Validation

- Compare section energy levels against the audible dynamics of the song.
- Confirm accent candidates cluster around strong onset activity.
- Confirm derived trends align with the section windows from Story 3.1.

## Failure Cases

- Too many accent candidates make the layer unusable downstream.
- Global statistics are copied into section cards instead of recalculated locally.
- Trend labels are unstable across repeated runs on the same input.

## Out of Scope

- Real-time adaptive lighting control
- Genre-specific drop classification beyond the shared trend vocabulary, which belongs to Story 4.5 song identifier inference
